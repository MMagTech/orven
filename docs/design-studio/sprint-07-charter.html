Orven Product Design Studio · Sprint 07

The charter, draft 1

A deliberate pause. Sprint 06 proved the language reaches every room — and quietly began confusing one implementation with the language itself. This document separates the two: what is law, what is Orven's chosen grammar, and what was merely furniture we happened to build on a Tuesday.

Part one

Three layers, three speeds of change

Everything we have designed sits at one of three altitudes, and the drift you caught was work sliding down the stack without anyone saying so.

LawsNever change

What makes something Orven at all. Violating one produces a different product. Written in words, not pixels.

GrammarChange rarely, deliberately

Orven's chosen way of expressing the laws — the recognizable devices. Another product could obey the same laws with different grammar; this grammar is what makes Orven look like Orven.

FurnitureFree to vary

Per-room, per-medium arrangement: row layouts, groupings, control styles, column counts. Sprint 06's mockups live almost entirely here.

The test for placing anything: could a different designer, given only the layers above it, produce a different version that is still unmistakably Orven? If yes, it belongs below the line they worked from. If the room must look exactly like our mockup to feel right, we have mistaken furniture for law.

Part two

The laws

The six from Sprint 04, plus two that Sprint 06's rooms forced into the open. Laws are stated without reference to any visual device — that is what makes them laws.

I
The edition, not the feed

Every Orven document is finite, dated, and numbered, with a beginning and a declared end. Nothing is infinite, live, or asking you back early.

II
Prominence is arithmetic

Whatever form prominence takes — size, order within a tier, space — it derives from countable fact, and the count is shown. Never from judged importance.

III
Two voices, one of them quiet

The report speaks in full declarative sentences; the apparatus labels in a smaller, plainer voice. No third voice — no icons, badges, or alarm color — ever speaks.

IV
Silence has a reserved seat

Absence of data is stated, structurally, wherever it occurs — in the brief, in history, in a source's record. It is never a footnote and never an implied all-clear.

V
The frame certifies

A fixed apparatus — identity, date, number, accounting — appears identically on the heaviest and emptiest days. Ritual and reassurance live in the frame so the content never performs either.

VI
Content breathes; structure doesn't

Density expands and contracts with the day; order and apparatus never do. Position carries no verdict.

VII
Counts, never gradesNew — from the beat page

Orven may total what the reader can verify ("filed in 213 of 217 editions"). It may never compress a record into a score, percentage grade, streak, or rating — a grade is a judgment wearing a number's clothes.

VIII
The reader's actions are facts tooNew — from settings

When the reader changes something, Orven records it as it records everything: attributed, dated, stated plainly ("Paused by you, 12 July"), taking effect at the next edition. Even the instrument's room is written, and nothing in it advises.

Part three

The grammar — chosen, not eternal

Sprint 05's six devices are Orven's signature expressions of the laws. They are identity, so they change only deliberately — but each has a revisable part, and naming it keeps us honest.

DeviceThe law it expressesWhat remains revisable
The bounds
Law I, V

The edition lives between an opening and closing mark; the document declares its own extent.

Revisable

That the mark is a double rule. The boundary is law; the rule style is signature.

The certification line
Law V

Identity · date · number · accounting, one quiet line, every document.

Revisable

Its exact composition and position — not its presence or its constancy.

The manifest
Law II, V

The whole before the parts: every source and its count, up front, as public arithmetic.

Revisable

Its form (line, block, list) and its mobile truncation rules.

Two inks
Law III

Reading serif for the report, quiet sans for the apparatus; the inversion in rooms where the reader acts.

Revisable

The specific faces. Georgia-class and Segoe-class are today's choices, not the identity.

Size as measurement
Law II

A small fixed scale of prominence steps, assigned by count alone.

Revisable

The number of steps (three) and the tier thresholds.

The reserved seat & the end
Law I, IV

Silence in the design's only enclosure; an explicit end with self-accounting.

Revisable

The box's placement per room; the end mark's wording.

Part four

Your three questions, answered honestly

The Archive

Fundamentally: the unbroken chronological record of editions — emphatically not "one row per edition."

Law

Chronological and complete; every edition present with equal standing — a quiet day is a full citizen of the record, never elided or collapsed. The archive states its own extent ("217 editions since 14 March"). No aggregation that ranks: a "busiest days" view would break Law II; a completion-streak would break Law VII.

Free

Rows, a calendar, a bound monthly volume, a spine of dates — any chronological form. How much of each morning shows per entry (headline, counts, manifest) is furniture, tunable per medium.

Sprint 06 chose

One row per edition, month labels, lead headline as caption. A good instance; only an instance.

The Beat page

Fundamentally: the public record of a reporting source — not a newspaper section.

Law

Three things, always: the source's credentials (what it observes, since when, how faithfully — as counts, per Law VII); its record as dated sentences; and its silences seated chronologically within that record (Law IV). Never a reliability grade, never a health status.

Free

Grouping (by edition, by week), depth shown before the archive takes over, whether credentials read as a passport block or a single line. Nothing requires it to resemble the Brief's own page.

Sprint 06 chose

Edition-by-edition rows, a "coverage" box confessing all four gaps ever. The full-confession box is furniture — the law only requires gaps to be visible in place.

Settings

Fundamentally: the room where the reader acts — and the identity there is the writing, not the widgetry.

Law

Every choice explained in a plain sentence of consequence; every change recorded as an attributed, dated fact; changes take effect at the next edition, and the page says so (Law VIII). The two-voice rule holds with the sans leading, because this is the instrument's room.

Free

The controls themselves. Conventional, immediately operable controls are not a betrayal — friction is a cost paid by a person, and the laws say nothing about underlines. If a standard select reduces friction, use it; the sentences around it carry the identity.

Sprint 06 chose

Underlined values, no visible controls at rest. Elegant, possibly too precious — this is the mockup most likely to have mistaken furniture for language, and your instinct to flag it was right.

The quiet edition and the night ink extract cleanly: the quiet day's law is Law V (the frame certifies, presence stated); "the manifest names beats on quiet days" is grammar-adjacent but honestly furniture. The night's law is constitutional — colors only; the blue-black palette itself is a choice, revisable.

For your review

Ratification

  1. Laws I–VIII: would you strike, amend, or add any? These are the sentences the implementation conversation will be governed by, so they deserve your red pen.
  2. Laws VII and VIII are new — inferred from your rooms rather than chosen by you. Do they read as discoveries or as my inventions?
  3. The grammar table's "revisable" column: is there a device whose revisable part you'd rather freeze (the double rule as permanent signature, say)?
  4. On settings: the charter says conventional controls are permitted and the sentences carry the identity. Does that match your instinct?
  5. Going forward, mockups will be labeled by layer — law, grammar, or furniture. Shall we resume the rooms on those terms, or is there another part of the language you want interrogated first?
