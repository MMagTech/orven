Orven Product Design Studio · Sprint 05

The distillation

Every element of the Broadsheet, tried individually on one question: does it improve a briefing, or does it exist because newspapers printed on paper? Then the survivors, reassembled — and proven as one brief across desktop, phone, and the printed page.

Part one

The tribunal

Each verdict was reached against all three mediums at once. The pattern that emerged: what survives is grammar — what falls is geometry. Newspapers solved "many facts, finite space, reader in a hurry" (Orven's exact problem) and also "cheap ink, expensive paper, newsstand competition" (not Orven's problem). We keep the first inheritance and decline the second.

Masthead

For the briefing: the brief needs an opening ceremony — a fixed signal that the document has begun. Paper nostalgia: its newsstand scale. A 40px letterspaced name exists to out-shout rival papers on a rack. Orven competes with no one on your own server.

Transform
Edition number & dateline

For the briefing: genuinely load-bearing. The date scopes every fact; the number certifies continuity — No. 215 tells you 214 came before and nothing slipped between. This is finiteness and self-accounting in four words.

Keep
“Inside” summary

For the briefing: the counts are not navigation — they are content: the shape of the night in one line, and the public arithmetic behind the layout. Paper nostalgia: the word "Inside," which assumes page-turning. It becomes the manifest — same line, honest name.

Transform
Horizontal rules

For the briefing: two rules earn their place — the double rule that opens the brief and the one that closes it. The document literally lives between them. Paper nostalgia: everything else. Newspapers ruled everywhere because ink was cheap and whitespace cost paper; on screen, whitespace is free and calmer. One boundary pair, a hairline under the manifest, dotted leaders in the record — nothing more.

Transform
Multi-column body text

Paper nostalgia, almost entirely. Snaking columns exist because a broadsheet page was too wide to read across. On desktop they force the eye to climb back up; on a phone they are impossible; only print can justify them. Side-by-side stories (parallel placement on wide media) survive; snaking text does not.

Drop
Justified text & hyphenation

Paper nostalgia. Justification is a narrow-column device; on screen it opens rivers of whitespace and adds visual tension. Ragged right is quieter — and calm outranks tidiness of edge.

Drop
Headline typography

For the briefing: this is the volume rule made visible — size as a unit of measurement, and a declarative sentence you can stop reading after. The essential Orven invention of Sprints 02–03. Tuned: newspaper scale-drama (27px shouting) compresses to a measured three-step scale.

Keep
Italic decks

Paper nostalgia. The deck was the newsstand's second sales pitch. A brief has no second pitch: the headline states the fact, the body gives detail. Two summary layers above every story is one more than a calm document needs.

Drop
Serif typefaces

For the briefing: serifs are reading typography, not paper typography — warmth and long-form trust. Paper nostalgia: Times/Cambria specifically, which is the newspaper costume itself. Resolved as two inks: a screen-bred serif (Georgia-class) for the report's voice; a quiet sans for the apparatus — labels, counts, times, frame. The report speaks; the instrument labels.

Transform
Section dividers & kickers

For the briefing: the kicker with its count ("Containers · 6 observations") is the audit trail of the whole design — it stays. Paper nostalgia: the heavy black bar above each one. On screen, space does the dividing; the label does the naming.

Transform
Boxed silence

For the briefing: the one enclosure in the whole design, reserved for missing coverage. The box is meaning, not decoration — absence made structural. Identical in all three mediums.

Keep
End of the brief

For the briefing: the closing boundary is the edition principle embodied — the document certifies its own completeness and states its accounting. Paper nostalgia: the fleuron ❦. Ornament borrowed from letterpress; the double rule and the words are enough.

Keep
Cream paper tint

Half and half. A near-white with a slight warm bias is screen comfort and deliberate neutrality — chosen, not inherited. Parchment yellow would be costume. Print resolves it honestly: real paper is whatever paper it is, so print sets pure black on white.

Transform

Part two

What survived: the Orven grammar

Not a newspaper anymore — a document grammar of six devices. Any medium that can draw a rule and set two typefaces can speak it.

The bounds

One double rule opens the brief, one closes it. Everything between them is the edition; nothing outside them exists.

The certification line

Wordmark, date, edition number, observation count — one quiet line, identical every morning, heavy or empty.

The manifest

Every beat and its count, one line, before anything else. The night's shape at a glance and the layout's public arithmetic.

Two inks

A reading serif for the facts; a small quiet sans for the apparatus — labels, counts, times. The report speaks, the instrument labels.

Size as measurement

Three headline steps assigned by count alone. Single reading column, ragged right; parallel stories sit side by side only when the medium is wide.

The reserved seat & the end

Silence in the design's only box. Then the closing rule: END OF THE BRIEF, with the edition's own accounting.

For your review

The distillation test

  1. Cover the wordmark and squint: is this still unmistakably a daily brief — the instant read you valued — without the newspaper costume?
  2. Any tribunal verdict you'd overturn? (The decks and justified columns are the two I'd expect you might miss.)
  3. Mobile truncates the manifest and collapses parallel stories to headlines. Acceptable adaptation, or does it break "the same Brief everywhere"?
  4. Print sets pure black on white while screens keep the warm near-white. One grammar, two papers — honest, or inconsistent?
  5. Does anything in the distilled version feel missing — a place where the newspaper's warmth did work we haven't replaced?
