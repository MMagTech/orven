Orven Product Design Studio · Sprint 04

The principles, proven

If the Broadsheet only works because it looks like a newspaper, we found a costume, not an identity. Below: the six principles extracted from Sprints 01–03, then the same frantic night — edition No. 217, 47 observations — rendered three ways that share nothing with a newspaper. If all three still feel like Orven, the principles are the identity, and the newspaper is just one garment it can wear.

Six principles, no layout attached

I.The edition, not the feed

A finite, numbered, dated artifact with a beginning and an explicit end. Never infinite, never live, never asking you back before tomorrow.

II.Prominence is arithmetic

Space follows the count of observations, never judgment. The count is printed beside the claim so the reader can audit the layout every morning.

III.Type is the only voice

No icons, badges, or alarm color. Hierarchy is carried by scale, weight, and rules; every fact is a declarative sentence with a time.

IV.Silence has a reserved seat

Missing coverage is a fixed structural element with its own enclosure — stated, never implied as all-clear, never a footnote.

V.The frame certifies

A fixed ceremonial apparatus — title, date, edition number, and a colophon that states the edition's own accounting — identical on heavy and quiet days.

VI.The page breathes; the order doesn't

Density expands and contracts with the day. Section order never changes, so position carries no verdict.

Proof A

The Drawing

An engineering sheet: the night surveyed to scale.

An engineer's drawing of the night. Each beat is a zone whose area is drawn to the scale of its count — prominence-as-arithmetic made literal, measurable with a ruler. Silence is a hatched zone, the draftsman's mark for "not surveyed." The title block — unchanged on every sheet — carries the certification: edition, date, totals, next issue.

Proof B

The Programme

An order of service: the night performed, movement by movement.

A concert programme for the night just performed. Each beat is a work; its observations are movements. Busy works list many movements; quiet works list one. A plugin that did not report is marked tacet — the musician's term for "silent this performance," which states absence without a whisper of alarm. The programme ends the way programmes always have: fine.

Proof C

The Timetable

A railway board: the night as a printed service.

A printed railway timetable. Each beat is a line; each observation a scheduled call with its time in the left column. A heavy night is simply a line with many calls — the block grows taller and the eye measures it instantly. A line not running is posted in the notice box, the way stations have always announced it: plainly, in the same ink as everything else.

For your review

The question this sprint answers

  1. Do all three still feel like Orven to you? If yes, the six principles are the identity — and the choice of garment (newspaper, drawing, programme, timetable) becomes a separate, smaller decision.
  2. Does any of these three make you hesitate on the Broadsheet — or do they confirm it?
  3. The Timetable's time-first left column is the one structural idea here the Broadsheet lacks. Worth stealing into the Broadsheet's grammar, or leave it?
  4. Which single principle of the six feels most essential — the one you would defend if you could only keep one?
